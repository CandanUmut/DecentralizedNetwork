# VISION — why this network exists

## The problem, plainly

Almost everything we do together online passes through someone else's computer. The chat
between you and your neighbor crosses a datacenter on another continent. The record of
who helped whom in your community — if it exists at all — lives in a spreadsheet on one
person's laptop, or in a company's database that can be sold, leaked, or shut down. The
"points" and "coins" of the internet are either corporate loyalty tokens (worthless
outside the store) or speculative assets (worth whatever a market panic says today).

None of that is how a healthy community actually works. Communities run on something
older: *I helped you, you remember it, and someday someone helps me.* That memory —
informal, unfair, forgettable — is the oldest ledger in the world. This project gives it
an honest, durable, shared home that no one owns.

## What this is

**A network of members' own computers** that keeps one shared, tamper-proof record of
who helped whom — plus encrypted messaging and member-to-member file storage — with **no
company, no server, no ads, and no way to buy your way in.**

Three ideas make it different from everything it superficially resembles:

1. **TimeCoin is created only by gratitude.** There is exactly one mint operation: a
   member signs "this person helped me, credit them." You cannot mint to yourself. Every
   coin traces back to a named act of help by a named voucher. And every member's thanks
   can create only a fixed amount per day — the community's *daily allowance*, agreed at
   the network's birth. Scarce like time, not like gold: everyone gets the same day.

2. **Trust is a web of real people, and it's revocable.** You vouch, on the record, for
   people you actually know. Your view of the economy counts only coin minted inside
   your vouch-neighborhood. A stranger — or a bot farm — can mint whatever it likes in
   its own corner; inside your community, that coin buys nothing, pays for nothing, and
   counts for nothing. When trust was misplaced, you revoke it, and the whole branch
   grafted onto that trust goes with it.

3. **Services are between members, paid in the community's own coin.** Today: encrypted
   direct messages (free — the network *is* the messenger) and file storage (a member
   with spare disk stores your data for TimeCoin, and you can spot-check they still hold
   it). Tomorrow, the same pattern extends to computation, backups, simulations —
   anything a community's machines can do for each other. Earning is contribution;
   spending is asking for help. The loop closes.

## Why decentralized, really

Not for ideology. For three concrete properties nothing centralized can give you:

- **Durability without a custodian.** The record replicates to every member. There is no
  server to fail, no company to fold, no admin to burn out. A koop's ledger should not
  die because its treasurer got busy.
- **Rules that can't be quietly changed.** The economy — how coin is created, how much,
  by whom — is baked into the network's genesis and enforced by every member's own
  machine. Changing the rules means visibly starting a new network, not editing a
  database row.
- **Privacy by architecture.** Messages travel encrypted from your machine to theirs.
  There is no middle to subpoena, monetize, or leak — because there is no middle.

## What it could become

Start with one koop, one family, one neighborhood: a favor ledger with teeth. Then the
same fabric carries the co-op's shared purchases, the community's document archive
stored across members' disks, mutual aid across towns via bridging members who belong to
both webs of trust. Further out — the original dream — communities pooling *computation*:
simulations, models, shared archives, run on member machines and paid in the coin of
contribution. The infrastructure below never has to change; each layer is just a new
protocol and a payment convention on the same ledger.

## What we will not do

- **No speculation.** TimeCoin is deliberately community-scoped and worthless to
  outsiders. The moment a coin like this trades for dollars, faking gratitude becomes an
  industry. Its non-convertibility is the security model, not a limitation.
- **No proof-of-work, no staking, no token sale.** The scarce resource here is standing
  with people who know you. That cannot be bought, burned, or delegated — which is the
  point.
- **No pretending.** The docs in this repo (README, ROADMAP, ASSESSMENT) state plainly
  what is verified, what is weak, and what is unsolved. Today's honest limits: it works
  among people with baseline trust in each other; it is young, unaudited software; a
  determined insider can still game edges of it until the web of trust reacts. It is a
  tool for communities, not armor against enemies.

## The one-sentence version

**A community's own internet: its memory of mutual help made durable and fair, its
money minted only by gratitude, its messages and files carried by its members'
machines — owned by everyone in it and no one above it.**
